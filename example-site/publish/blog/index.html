Stories from the bakery floor — what we're baking, what we're testing, and what went sideways along the way.

We're at the Farmers Market This Weekend

Look for the Hearth & Wheat tent at the Rivermill Farmers Market Saturday morning, 8am until we sell out (usually by 10). Ida's running the table with a smaller lineup than the shop: sourdough boules, the cinnamon-walnut morning buns, and a tray of day-old pastries at half price for anyone up early enough to beat the rush.

Cash or card both work this year — we finally caved and got a reader that doesn't die every time it's cloudy.

If it rains, we're still there. Bread doesn't care about weather.

Maren Takes Home the Rivermill Blue Ribbon — Again

Two years running now: Maren entered her country sourdough in the Rivermill Harvest Fair baking competition and walked away with the blue ribbon, beating out eleven other bakeries from three counties.

Her secret, if you can call it that, is no secret at all — she's been feeding the same starter since she took over the bakery in 2011, and she still shapes every competition loaf by hand instead of the mixer. "Judges can taste when you rush a dough," she says. "So don't."

The winning loaf is back in the case this week in limited numbers, labeled "Fair Winner" so you know which one to grab.

New Ovens, Same Starter

We finally retired the two cranky electric decks that have been limping along since 2011 and swapped in a proper stone-hearth deck oven — same wood-fired soul as the original, just enough room to bake twice the loaves at once.

The good news: our sourdough starter (yes, the one older than Maren's kids) didn't notice a thing. First bake out of the new oven had the same crackling crust and open crumb we've been chasing for fifteen years. If anything, the rye came out even better — more even heat means fewer scorched bottoms for whoever's on the early shift.

Come say hello to the new setup. We're celebrating with a free slice of whatever's cooling on the rack, while it lasts.

Rye Season Returns

Every year around this time we bring back our dark rye — the one with caraway, a spoonful of molasses, and a crumb dense enough to hold up a week of sandwiches. It disappears fast, so if you see it in the case, it's not going to be there tomorrow.

We started making this loaf back when Ingrid ran the ovens alone on Elm Street, adjusting the molasses by feel depending on how the flour was behaving that week. We still do it that way. No scale for that part — just Theo, a spoon, and a lot of muscle memory.

Ask at the counter if you want a heel end for tasting before you commit to a whole loaf.
